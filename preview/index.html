Presidential Biographies: The Challenges Then and Now

The explorations of journalists and historians of a president's leadership and abilities to communicate with the American people have often provided the undergirding for the functioning of our democracy. This panel discusses the significance of what we've learned from some of the best, all of whom have won Pulitzers.

Speakers
Jon Meacham | Panelist

Presidential historian, contributing editor at TIME, and Pulitzer Prize-winning author Jon Meacham is executive vice president and executive editor at Random House. Meacham’s latest book, Destiny and Power: The American Odyssey of George Herbert Walker Bush, was published by Random House on November 10, 2015. His book, American Lion: Andrew Jackson in the White House, was a New York Times bestseller and awarded the Pulitzer Prize for Biography in 2009. After serving as managing editor of Newsweek for eight years, Meacham was responsible for all day-to-day editorial operations of the magazine as editor from 2006 to 2010. He lives in Nashville and in Sewanee with his wife and children.

Annette Gordon-Reed | Panelist

Annette Gordon-Reed is the Charles Warren Professor of American Legal History at Harvard Law School, a professor of history in the Faculty of Arts and Sciences at Harvard University, the Carol K. Pforzheimer Professor at the Radcliffe Institute for Advanced Study and formerly the Harold Vyvyan Harmsworth Visiting Professor of American History at the Queen’s College, University of Oxford (2014-2015). Gordon-Reed won the Pulitzer Prize in History in 2009 for The Hemingses of Monticello: An American Family (W.W. Norton, 2009), and her forthcoming book is “’Most Blessed of Patriarchs’: Thomas Jefferson and the Empire of the Imagination” (with Peter S. Onuf).

Ron Chernow | Panelist

An honors graduate of Yale and Cambridge, Ron Chernow is one of the most distinguished commentators on history, politics, business, and finance in America today. His books, The House of Morgan, The Warburgs, Titan and Alexander Hamilton, were met with critical acclaim and numerous awards. His long­ awaited biography of George Washington, Washington: A Life, was honored with the Pulitzer Prize in Biography. Since 2009, Chernow has worked with Lin­-Manuel Miranda as the historical consultant on the sensational Broadway musical “Hamilton,” which was inspired by his biography of Alexander Hamilton. He resides in Brooklyn, New York.

Mark Updegrove | Moderator

Mark K. Updegrove, is the director of the LBJ Presidential Library in Austin, Texas, and the author of four books on the presidency. An analyst for ABC News on matters relating to U.S. politics and the presidency, Mr. Updegrove has written for American Heritage, The Daily Beast, The Nation, National Geographic, Parade, Politico, Texas Monthly, and Time. He has conducted exclusive interviews with five U.S. Presidents.

The Presidency: Coverage in the Digital Age

In the digital age, when anyone can tweet a breaking news headline or blog a public-affairs anyalysis, news organizations are changing how they cover news. Similarly, political leaders make use of digital tools, including social media, to bypass traditional media "gatekeepers" and communicate directly with targeted, usually like-minded, people. This panel looks at the impact of this digital phenomenon on coverage, presidential action and public opinion.

Speakers
Kathleen Carroll | Panelist

Kathleen Carroll, executive editor and senior vice president of The Associated Press, is the top news executive of the world’s largest independent news agency, a role she has held since 2002. She is responsible for news content in all formats from the journalists based in more than 280 bureaus and 110 countries. She is an industry leader on government challenges to press freedom and on vital security issues for journalists in war zones and other hostile environments. AP journalists have won numerous awards during her tenure, including 4 Pulitzer Prizes and 6 George Polk Awards. She is vice-chair of the Committee to Protect Journalists board and was a member of the Pulitzer Prize Board from 2003-2012, the last year as co-chair. Formerly a reporter at The Dallas Morning News, she is a Texas native.

Mo Elleithee | Panelist

Mo Elleithee recently was named the founding executive director of Georgetown University’s Institute of Politics and Public Service. The former communications director of the Democratic National Committee, he is a veteran of four presidential campaigns, including as senior spokesman on Hillary Clinton’s 2008 campaign. He was a campaign strategist for four current or former U.S. senators and two former governors, advised the independent expenditure campaign arms of both the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee and Democratic Senatorial Campaign Committee, and has worked on numerous other statewide and local races in every region of the country. Elleithee earned his undergraduate degree from Georgetown University’s School of Foreign Service, and an M.A. in political management from the George Washington University.

Oliver Knox | Panelist

Olivier Knox is Chief Washington Correspondent for Yahoo News, where he has covered the White House, politics and foreign policy since February 2012. Before that, he was at Agence France­-Presse for 15 years, covering stories like the impeachment of Bill Clinton and Al Gore's 2000 campaign. He covered the White House for all eight years of George W. Bush's presidency. He got his BA in International Affairs from Columbia University and his MA in Latin American Studies from the Johns Hopkins School of Advanced International Studies. He grew up in Middlebury, Vermont and Paris, France, and currently lives just outside of Washington with his wife and son.

Kevin Sullivan | Panelist

As Senior Advisor for External Affairs, Kevin Sullivan leads communications and marketing efforts across all areas of the Bush Center, including the Bush Institute and the Bush Presidential Library and Museum. Sullivan was appointed by President George W. Bush as Assistant to the President for Communications in July 2006, where he oversaw message development and communications planning. Prior to working in the White House, Sullivan served as the Senate-confirmed Assistant Secretary for Communications and Outreach at the U.S. Department of Education, and in senior leadership roles with NBC Universal, NBC Sports and the Dallas Mavericks. He and his wife, Jo Anne, have five children.

Marty Baron | Moderator

Martin “Marty” Baron became executive editor of The Washington Post in 2013. He oversees the Post’s print and digital news operations and a staff of about 700 journalists. He has been a part of ten Pulitzer Prizes during his time at The Post, The Boston Globe, The New York Times, The Los Angeles Times and The Miami Herald. Baron was named Editor of the Year by Editor & Publisher magazine in 2001, and Editor of the Year by the National Press Foundation in 2004. Raised in Tampa, FL, Baron speaks fluent Spanish. He graduated from Lehigh University in 1976 with BA and MBA degrees.

Presidents and Poverty: The Fight that Never Ends

A succession of presidents have made fighting poverty and helping impoverished Americans confront and overcome poverty a top issue of their administration. This panel features former administration officials and prize-winning journalists who have wrestled with these issues. We focus the discussion on domestic poverty and its implications, the growing income disparity in the U.S., poverty's impact on real people and how it shapes communities.

Speakers
Eli Saslow | Panelist

Eli Saslow is a journalist for The Washington Post, where he works on the national staff. In 2014, he won the Pulitzer Prize for a series on food stamps. He is a recipient of the George Polk award and other honors. He is author of Ten Letters: The Stories Americans Tell Their President (Random House, 2012). He also writes for ESPN The Magazine and three of his works have appeared in the anthology Best American Sports Writing. He lives with his wife and their three children in Portland, Oregon.

Sharon Grigsby | Panelist

After working at newspapers in New York and Detroit, Sharon Grigsby joined The Dallas Morning News as a young editor who expected to be back in Texas for only a year or two. But landing in the middle of one of the country’s most intensive newspaper wars, she went on to work in most every nook and cranny of the DMN, including national news, politics, features, religion and local news – culminating in running the Metro operation -- before joining the Editorial Board. She divides her time these days between writing editorials and directing the department’s “Bridging Dallas’ North-South Gap” project, which won the 2010 Pulitzer Prize for Editorial Writing.

John M. Bridgeland | Panelist

John Bridgeland is founder & CEO of Civic Enterprises; co-chair of The Service Year Alliance of The Aspen Institute to make a year of national service a common expectation and opportunity for all 18-28 year olds; co- convener of GradNation to reach a 90% high school graduation rate nationally by 2020; and vice chairman of Malaria No More, a nonprofit working to end malaria deaths in Africa. Previously, Bridgeland was appointed by President Obama to serve on the White House Council for Community Solutions. He served as director of the White House Domestic Policy Council, Assistant to the President of the United States and first director of the USA Freedom Corps under President George W. Bush.

Joseph Califano | Panelist

Joseph A. Califano, Jr. was President Lyndon B. Johnson’s chief domestic policy aide from 1965 until 1969 and Secretary of Health, Education and Welfare in the Carter Administration from 1977 to 1979. He was born in Brooklyn and received his BA from Holy Cross College in Worcester, Mass., and his LLB from Harvard Law School. He is the author of 13 books, founder of The National Center on Addiction and Substance Abuse, has practiced law in Washington, D.C. and New York City and is the parent of five and grandparent of nine. He and his wife Hilary live in Westport, Connecticut.

Eugene Robinson | Moderator

Eugene Robinson is columnist and a former assistant managing editor of The Washington Post. His columns are syndicated by The Washington Post Writers Group, and he won the 2009 Pulitzer Prize for Commentary. A native of South Carolina, he began his journalism career at The San Francisco Chronicle; his assignments included the trial of publishing heiress Patty Hearst. He later covered city hall for The Post and served as a foreign correspondent in Buenos Aires, Argentina, and London, England. He is a member of the National Association of Black Journalists and a board member of the International Women's Media Foundation.

The Right to Know vs. the Responsibility to Protect: How to Tell the Difference

The Guardianand Washington Post shared the 2014 Pulitzer Prize for Public Service for their exposure of the surveillance activities of the NSA following the leaks of Edward Snowden. Where does the people’s right to know rub up against government’s responsibility to protect? How do administration officials and journalists navigate these competing interests? It’s a natural tension that transcends party, administration, news organizations. What criteria do administration officials use to determine what to keep classified? Is transparency a double-edged sword? What sorts of guidelines should news organizations employ in discerning what’s newsworthy?

Speakers
Jill Abramson | Panelist

Jill Abramson is a journalist who spent the last 17 years in the most senior editorial positions at The New York Times, where she was the first woman to serve as Washington bureau chief, managing editor and executive editor. Before joining the Times, she was deputy Washington bureau chief and an investigative reporter covering money and politics at The Wall Street Journal for nine years. She is an author, lecturer, teacher and member of the American Academy of Arts & Sciences and The American Philosophical Society.

Len Downie | Panelist

Leonard Downie Jr., the Weil Family Professor of Journalism at Arizona State University’s Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication, was executive editor of The Washington Post from 1991 to 2008. During his 44 years in The Washington Post newsroom, he was also an investigative reporter, editor of the local and national news staffs, London correspondent, and, from 1984 to 1991, managing editor under then executive editor Ben Bradlee. As deputy Metro editor from 1972 to 1974, Downie helped supervise the newspaper’s Watergate coverage. He oversaw the newspaper’s coverage of every national election from 1984 through 2008. During his 17 years as executive editor, The Washington Post news staff won 25 Pulitzer prizes. Downie received Bachelor’s, Master’s and honorary Doctor of Humane Letters degrees from The Ohio State University.

Leon Panetta | Panelist

Leon E. Panetta is the co-founder and chairman of The Panetta Institute for Public Policy and former Secretary of Defense. A Monterey native and Santa Clara University School of Law graduate, Secretary Panetta’s long and distinguished public service career has spanned the U.S. Army, U.S. House of Representatives, Office of Management and Budget, President Clinton’s Chief of Staff, Director of the CIA and 23rd Secretary of Defense. Presently, Secretary Panetta serves on the boards of directors for Oracle, Blue Shield of California and the Commission on Presidential Debates.

Gen. Michael Hayden | Panelist

General Michael Hayden is a Principal at The Chertoff Group, a premier global advisory firm that focuses exclusively on the security and risk management sector by providing consulting, mergers and acquisitions (M&A), and risk management services to clients seeking to secure and grow their enterprises. At The Chertoff Group, Hayden uses his broad geographic and political knowledge to brief clients on intelligence matters worldwide – including any developments in cybersecurity that may affect their businesses. Hayden served as the director of the CIA from 2006 to 2009, where he was responsible for overseeing all information concerning the plans, intentions and capabilities of America’s adversaries, producing timely analysis for decision makers, and conducting covert operations to thwart terrorists and other enemies of the US

Steve Coll | Moderator

Steve Coll is a staff writer at The New Yorker, the author of seven books of nonfiction, and a two-time winner of the Pulitzer Prize. Between 1985 and 2005, he was a reporter, foreign correspondent, senior editor and managing editor (1998-2004) at The Washington Post. In 2004, he joined The New Yorker, where he has written on international politics, American politics and national security, intelligence controversies and the media. Coll served as president of the New America Foundation, a public-policy institute in Washington, D.C., from 2007 to 2013, when he was named dean of Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism.

The Politics of Polarization: Can Democracy Survive?

Are American politics today more polarized than ever before? Why is that? Or if not, why does it seem so? Is this good or bad? Is our democracy eroding in the face of demographic fracturing, increased class warfare and exacerbating fear and distrust? What can/should our leaders do about it? What can we do about it?

Speakers
Bret Stephens | Panelist

Bret Stephens writes "Global View," the Wall Street Journal's principal column on foreign affairs, for which he won the 2013 Pulitzer Prize for commentary. He is the paper's deputy editorial page editor, responsible for its global opinion pages, and a member of the Editorial Board. Previously, he was editor-­in-­chief of The Jerusalem Post. His book, America in Retreat: The New Isolationism and the Coming Global Disorder was recently reissued in paperback by Sentinel Books. He lives in New York City with his wife Corinna, a classical music critic for the New York Times, and their three children.

Nolan McCarty | Panelist

Nolan McCarty is the Susan Dod Brown Professor of Politics and Public Affairs and Chair of the Department of Politics. He was formerly the associate dean at the Woodrow Wilson School of Public and International Affairs. His research interests include U.S. politics, democratic political institutions, and political game theory. He is the recipient of the Robert Eckles Swain National Fellowship from the Hoover Institution and the John M. Olin Fellowship in Political Economy. He has co­authored three books and, in 2010, was elected a fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences. He earned his A.B. from the University of Chicago and his PhD from Carnegie Mellon University. He was born in Odessa, Texas.

Mike McCurry | Panelist

Mike McCurry is a Distinguished Professor of Public Theology at the Wesley Theological Seminary in Washington, DC and a partner at Public Strategies Washington, Inc., where he provides counsel on communications strategies and management to corporate and non-profit clients. McCurry is a veteran political strategist and spokesperson with nearly four decades of experience in the nation’s capital. McCurry served in the White House as press secretary to President Bill Clinton (1995-1998), spokesman for the U.S. Department of State (1993-1995) and director of communications for the Democratic National Committee (1988-1990). He is co-chairman of the Commission on Presidential Debates, which sponsors the general election debates between candidates for President and Vice President of the United States.

Danielle Allen | Moderator

Danielle Allen is director of the Edmond J. Safra Center for Ethics at Harvard University and professor in Harvard’s Department of Government and Graduate School of Education. She is a political theorist who has published broadly in democratic theory, political sociology, and the history of political thought, and an author known for her work on justice and citizenship in both ancient Athens and modern America. She is chair of the Mellon Foundation Board, past chair of the Pulitzer Prize Board, and a member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and American Philosophical Society.
